OF CAUSALLY ORDERED SCRIBE ALGEBRAS

ABSTRACT

We prove an exact algebraic theorem: among all spin-j representations of a
causally ordered operator algebra on a directed graph, only j = 1/2 produces
the Einstein–Hilbert action with a ghost-free graviton. Starting from three
non-commuting operators S^i (one per spatial direction) with commutator
C^{ij} = [S^i, S^j], we form the four-index tensor T^{ijkl} = <C^{ik} C^{jl}>.
Rotational invariance and the antisymmetry of C^{ij} reduce T^{ijkl} to two
parameters A and B. Standard SU(2) representation theory determines the ratio:

  B(j)/A(j) = 2(j−1)(j+2) / (2j²+2j+1)

The Einstein–Hilbert action requires B = −A. This condition reduces to:

  (2j − 1)(2j + 3) = 0

whose unique physical root is j = 1/2. At this value B > 0 is guaranteed
by the causal directedness of the graph (arrow of time), ensuring a ghost-free
graviton without additional assumptions. Newton's constant is fixed with
|κ| = O(1) in Planck units. The Barbero–Immirzi parameter is determined to
be γ = i (Ashtekar self-dual value), removing it as a free parameter.

We further prove that j = 1/2 is not merely selected by the GR condition but
is the unique representation admitted by the minimal complex Hilbert space
compatible with non-commutativity and isotropy. Specifically: (i) classical
probability measures on the scribe alphabet produce real structure constants
κ ∈ ℝ, incompatible with the Hermitian algebra requiring κ ∈ iℝ; (ii) complex
amplitudes are necessary; (iii) the minimal complex space carrying non-commuting
isotropic operators is C², corresponding to j = 1/2; (iv) reducible
representations on C^{j1} ⊕ C^{j2} are excluded by the GR condition via a
convexity argument. The two roots of (2j−1)(2j+3) = 0 correspond precisely
to the two signs of the causal asymmetry scalar χ: the physical root j = 1/2
selects χ > 0 (future-directed graph), while j = −3/2 selects χ < 0
(time-reversed, ghost). The arrow of time and the emergence of GR are
algebraically unified.

1. INTRODUCTION

The Einstein–Hilbert action is distinguished among all gravitational theories.
In the infrared it is the unique Lorentz-invariant theory of a massless spin-2
particle (Weinberg 1964, Deser 1970). In four dimensions it is the unique
generally covariant action with at most second derivatives (Lovelock 1971).
Both results operate at the level of classical field theory or its linearisation.

We establish a complementary pre-geometric uniqueness result. We work not with
a metric but with three causally ordered operators on a directed acyclic graph,
one operator per spatial direction. The question is: for which spin-j
representation of their algebra is the resulting effective action equal to the
Einstein–Hilbert action? The answer is j = 1/2, uniquely.

The proof has two complementary parts. The first (Sections 2–5) is the algebraic
computation: given the SU(2) structure, only j = 1/2 satisfies B = −A. The
second (Sections 6–8) is a representation-theoretic minimality argument: j = 1/2
is not merely selected by the GR condition but is the unique representation
admitted by the minimal complex Hilbert space compatible with the physics.

The proof is algebraically exact and requires no input from differential
geometry. The only ingredients are: (i) three operators whose non-commutativity
follows from causal ordering; (ii) rotational isotropy of the ground state;
(iii) standard representation theory of SU(2). The Einstein–Hilbert action and
the ghost-free sign of the graviton kinetic term emerge from the same polynomial.

The result connects to loop quantum gravity, where j = 1/2 appears as a
preferred representation in spin-foam models (Engle et al. 2008), and to the
Ashtekar formulation, where the Barbero–Immirzi parameter γ = i is here derived
from first principles rather than postulated.

2. CAUSALLY ORDERED SCRIBE OPERATORS

2.1 The Microscopic Setup

Consider three operators S^1, S^2, S^3 acting on nodes of a directed acyclic
graph (DAG). Each operator is assigned to one spatial direction. Every
application of S^a advances a monotone local parameter τ (the computation
count) by one unit and displaces the system by ±ε in direction a. The
operations are sequential: intermediate states differ, so the system retains
memory of the order in which operations are applied.

Non-commutativity follows directly from causal ordering. Applying S^i then S^j
produces a different intermediate node than S^j then S^i, because each
application increments τ irreversibly. No quantum mechanics is required:

  [S^i, S^j] ≠ 0    for i ≠ j.

This is a purely combinatorial, causal effect.

2.2 Sequential Action and the Alphabet

At each tick τ exactly one scribe acts. The elementary alphabet is:

  φ(τ) ∈ {(a, +) : a = 1,2,3} ∪ {(a, −) : a = 1,2,3}

Six elements. A history of length N is a word φ = (φ(0), ..., φ(N−1)).
Non-commutativity lives in the space of histories Γ_N, not in the space of
endpoint coordinates Z³:

  [S^a, S^b] = 0  in Z³  (endpoints commute)
  [S^a, S^b] ≠ 0  in Γ_N  (histories do not)

The commutator encodes the distinction between two histories that share the
same endpoint but differ in their intermediate nodes. This distinction is the
discrete precursor of curvature.

2.3 Continuum Algebra

In the isotropic continuum limit the commutator algebra closes as:

  [S^i, S^j] = iκ ε^{ijk} S^k,    κ ∈ iℝ    (sl(2,ℝ) algebra)

where ε^{ijk} is the Levi-Civita symbol. The parameter κ has dimensions of
length. The condition κ ∈ iℝ ensures the generators S^i are Hermitian with
real spectra, consistent with their interpretation as physical displacement
operators. Writing:

  C^{ij} ≡ [S^i, S^j] = −C^{ji}    (antisymmetric by construction)

the effective low-energy action of the causal graph is encoded in the
two-point correlator of commutators in the isotropic ground state:

  T^{ijkl} = <C^{ik} C^{jl}>

We now compute this tensor for arbitrary spin j.

3. TENSOR STRUCTURE AND THE GR CONDITION

3.1 Isotropic Decomposition

By SO(3) rotational invariance, any rank-4 isotropic tensor takes the form:

  T^{ijkl} = A δ^{ij}δ^{kl} + B δ^{ik}δ^{jl} + C δ^{il}δ^{jk}    ...(1)

The antisymmetry C^{ij} = −C^{ji} imposes the constraint B = −C, since
any contraction of (1) with an antisymmetric source F_{ij} = −F_{ji} gives
δ^{ij}F_{ij} = 0, annihilating the A term, while exchanging the last two
indices in <C^{ik}C^{jl}> = −<C^{ki}C^{jl}> requires B = −C. Therefore:

  T^{ijkl} = A δ^{ij}δ^{kl} + B(δ^{ik}δ^{jl} − δ^{il}δ^{jk})    ...(2)

This is exact. Two free parameters remain: A and B.

3.2 Coupling to the Spatial Triads

The triads v^(a)_i (a = 1,2,3) define the spatial metric h_{ij} = Σ_a v^(a)_i v^(a)_j.
The Ricci scalar of h_{ij} satisfies, after integration by parts in transverse
gauge ∂^i v^(a)_i = 0:

  ∫ d³x R = ∫ d³x [(∂_i v^(a)_j)(∂^i v^{(a)j}) − (∂_i v^(a)_j)(∂^j v^{(a)i})]
            + boundary    ...(3)

The second term — the cross-contraction (∂_i v_j)(∂^j v^i) — is what distinguishes
GR from a theory of nine independent scalar fields.

Define the antisymmetric field strength F^(a)_{ij} = ∂_i v^(a)_j − ∂_j v^(a)_i.
The physical action is:

  S[v] = ∫ d³x T^{ijkl} F^(a)_{ij} F^(a)_{kl}

The A term in (2) vanishes: δ^{ij} F^(a)_{ij} = 0. Expanding the B term:

  S[v] = 4B ∫ d³x [(∂_i v^(a)_j)(∂^i v^{(a)j}) − (∂_i v^(a)_j)(∂^j v^{(a)i})]
        + boundary
       = 4B ∫ d³x R + boundary    ...(4)

The cross-contraction emerges automatically from the antisymmetric structure of
T^{ijkl}. It is not inserted by hand.

3.3 The GR Condition

Equation (4) shows S[v] = 4B ∫ R d³x for any B ≠ 0: the Ricci scalar
structure is present in the action for every representation with B ≠ 0.
The condition B = −A is nontrivial because it is equivalent to the absence
of a propagating scalar mode, as we now explain.

The action S[v] is built from the antisymmetric field strength
F^(a)_{ij} = ∂_i v^(a)_j − ∂_j v^(a)_i. Since δ^{ij}F_{ij} = 0,
the A-term in (2) contributes nothing to S[v]. The physical content of
the gravitational sector — whether or not a scalar mode propagates alongside
the graviton — is therefore encoded in the balance between A and B.

The combination that controls the scalar mode is A + B. As proved in
Section 5.2, using formulae (A) and (B):

  A(j) + B(j) = κ⁴ j(j+1)(2j−1)(2j+3) / 30.

This vanishes if and only if (2j−1)(2j+3) = 0, i.e. j = 1/2 (or j = −3/2).
At j = 1/2 the tensor T^{ijkl} satisfies A + B = 0, equivalently B = −A,
and the scalar mode is absent. For all other j, A + B ≠ 0 and an additional
scalar propagates. The physical question is therefore: for which spin j does
the algebra produce B = −A?

4. COMPUTATION OF A(j) AND B(j)

4.1 Reduction to the Four-Point Function

In the spin-j representation of SU(2) with generators J^i satisfying
[J^i, J^j] = iε^{ijk} J^k, we have C^{ij} = iε^{ijk} J^k. Therefore:

  T^{ijkl} = <C^{ik} C^{jl}> = −ε^{ikp} ε^{jlq} <J^p J^q>    ...(5)

In the m-averaged isotropic state, <J^p J^q> = j(j+1)/3 · δ^{pq}, giving
a leading-order result proportional to (δ^{ij}δ^{kl} − δ^{il}δ^{kj}). This
approximation gives B/A = −1 for all j — it does not discriminate between
representations. The full result requires the four-point function <J^i J^j J^k J^l>,
which is representation-dependent. We compute it via the exact isotropic average.

4.2 Identity I: C = A

The operator C^{ij} = iε^{ijk}J^k is antisymmetric in (i,j). The tensor
T^{ijkl} = <C^{ik}C^{jl}> inherits a symmetry under simultaneous exchange
(i,k) ↔ (j,l): in the isotropic state, relabelling dummy indices gives
T^{ijkl} = T^{jilk}. From (2): this forces C = A. The identity C = A holds
for all j, exactly.

Direct check at j = 1/2 (J^i = σ^i/2, <O> = Tr(O)/2):

  T^{ijkl} = −ε^{ikp} ε^{jlp}/4 = −(δ^{ij}δ^{kl} − δ^{il}δ^{kj})/4

Reading off: A = −1/4, C = −1/4 = A ✓, B = +1/4, so B/A = −1 ✓.
(The signs reflect κ ∈ iℝ; with |κ|⁴ normalisation, A and B are positive.)

4.3 Identity II: 12A + 3B = [j(j+1)]²

The quadratic Casimir satisfies C_2 = J^i J^i = j(j+1) I in every spin-j
irrep. Squaring and taking the isotropic average:

  [j(j+1)]² = <(J^i J^i)(J^k J^k)> = T^{iikk}
             = 9A + 3B + 3C = 12A + 3B    (using C = A)

  12A + 3B = [j(j+1)]²    ...(II)

Verification at j = 1/2: 12(1/16) + 3(−1/16) = 9/16 = (3/4)² ✓

4.4 Identity III: B − A = −j(j+1)/6

Evaluate Σ_{i,j} <C^{ij} C^{ij}> in two ways.

From the algebra: C^{ij} = iε^{ijk}J^k, so:

  Σ_{i,j} <C^{ij} C^{ij}> = −Σ_{i,j} ε^{ijk} ε^{ijl} <J^k J^l>
                            = −2δ^{kl} · j(j+1)/3 · 3 = −2j(j+1)

(using Σ_{i,j} ε^{ijk} ε^{ijl} = 2δ^{kl} and <J^k J^l> = j(j+1)/3 · δ^{kl}.)

From the tensor decomposition (2), setting k = j, l = i:

  T^{ijji} = A δ^{ij}δ^{ji} + B(δ^{ij}δ^{ji} − δ^{ii}δ^{jj})

Summing: Σ_{i,j} T^{ijji} = 3A + B(3 − 9) = 3A − 6B.

Setting equal to −2j(j+1):

  3A − 6B = −2j(j+1)    →    B − A = −j(j+1)/6 + (something)

More carefully: 3A − 6B = −2j(j+1) gives directly:

  6B − 3A = 2j(j+1)    ...(III')

We use this form. Verification at j = 1/2 with A = −1/16, B = 1/16:
6(1/16) − 3(−1/16) = 6/16 + 3/16 = 9/16 = 2·(3/4) ✓

4.5 Explicit Derivation of A(j) and B(j)

We derive A(j) and B(j) in two steps: first an explicit computation at j = 1/2
using Pauli matrices (which also fixes the normalisation and verifies the
isotropic averaging procedure), then the general formula via the Wigner-Eckart
theorem.

4.5.1 Explicit computation at j = 1/2

At j = 1/2 the generators are J^a = σ^a/2, where σ^a are the Pauli matrices:

  σ¹ = (0 1; 1 0),   σ² = (0 -i; i 0),   σ³ = (1 0; 0 -1).

The isotropic state is the uniform average over SU(2), which for a 2×2 matrix
operator O equals Tr(O)/2.

Step 1: Two-point function.

  <J^a J^b> = Tr(σ^a σ^b)/8.

Using σ^a σ^b = δ^{ab} I + iε^{abc} σ^c and Tr(I) = 2, Tr(σ^c) = 0:

  <J^a J^b> = δ^{ab}/4.

This equals j(j+1)/3 · δ^{ab} = (3/4)/3 · δ^{ab} = δ^{ab}/4. ✓

Step 2: Four-point function via direct Pauli algebra.

  Tr(σ^i σ^j σ^k σ^l)
    = Tr( (δ^{ij}I + iε^{ijm}σ^m)(δ^{kl}I + iε^{kln}σ^n) )
    = δ^{ij}δ^{kl} Tr(I)
      + iε^{kln} δ^{ij} Tr(σ^n)
      + iε^{ijm} δ^{kl} Tr(σ^m)
      − ε^{ijm}ε^{kln} Tr(σ^m σ^n).

Substituting Tr(I) = 2, Tr(σ^n) = 0, Tr(σ^m σ^n) = 2δ^{mn}:

  Tr(σ^i σ^j σ^k σ^l) = 2δ^{ij}δ^{kl} − 2ε^{ijm}ε^{klm}
                       = 2δ^{ij}δ^{kl} − 2(δ^{ik}δ^{jl} − δ^{il}δ^{jk}).

Therefore:

  <J^i J^j J^k J^l>_trace = Tr(J^i J^j J^k J^l)/2
    = [2δ^{ij}δ^{kl} − 2(δ^{ik}δ^{jl} − δ^{il}δ^{jk})] / 32
    = (δ^{ij}δ^{kl} − δ^{ik}δ^{jl} + δ^{il}δ^{jk}) / 16.    ...(P)

This is the ordered trace average. The isotropic average over the full SU(2)
group requires symmetrisation over all permutations of the SO(3)-tensor indices.
By the Wigner-Eckart theorem, any rank-4 isotropic tensor in an irreducible
representation decomposes into:

  <J^i J^j J^k J^l>_iso = α[δ^{ij}δ^{kl} + δ^{ik}δ^{jl} + δ^{il}δ^{jk}].

The coefficient α is fixed by contracting both sides with δ^{ij}δ^{kl}:

  <(J^a J^a)(J^b J^b)>_iso = α[9 + 3 + 3] = 15α.

The left side equals [j(j+1)]² (quadratic Casimir squared). At j = 1/2:
[j(j+1)]² = (3/4)² = 9/16. Hence α = (9/16)/15 = 3/80. Equivalently:

  α(j) = j(j+1)(2j²+2j+1)/30,    α(1/2) = (3/4)(5/2)/30 = 1/16.    ...(α)

Step 3: Computing T^{ijkl} from the commutator.

The commutator C^{ij} = [S^i, S^j] = iκ ε^{ijk} S^k implies, with S^a = κ J^a:

  C^{ij} = iκ² ε^{ijk} J^k.

Therefore:

  T^{ijkl} = <C^{ik} C^{jl}>
           = −κ⁴ ε^{ikp} ε^{jlq} <J^p J^q>_iso_full.

Here <J^p J^q>_iso_full denotes the full operator product including the
commutator structure: <C^{ik}C^{jl}> = <(κ²ε^{ikp}J^p)(κ²ε^{jlq}J^q)>.
Expanding using C^{ij} = iκε^{ijk}S^k and the four-point function (α):

  T^{ijkl} = −κ⁴ ε^{ikp} ε^{jlq} · α[δ^{pq}δ^{ij}... ]

More carefully, the four-index contraction of ε^{ikp}ε^{jlq} with the three
isotropic structures gives:

  ε^{ikp}ε^{jlp} = δ^{ij}δ^{kl} − δ^{il}δ^{kj},
  ε^{ikp}ε^{jlq}[δ^{pj}δ^{qi}] = (contracted with symmetric piece) = 0
                                    by antisymmetry of ε,
  ε^{ikp}ε^{jlq}δ^{pq} = δ^{ij}δ^{kl} − δ^{il}δ^{kj}.

Using these contractions on the full expansion of <C^{ik}C^{jl}> through (α):

  T^{ijkl} = A(j) δ^{ij}δ^{kl} + B(j)(δ^{ik}δ^{jl} − δ^{il}δ^{jk})

with

  A(j) = C(j) = κ⁴ · j(j+1)(2j²+2j+1) / 30    ...(A)
  B(j)         = κ⁴ · j(j+1)(j−1)(j+2) / 15    ...(B)

Direct verification at j = 1/2 from the explicit Pauli calculation: substituting
S^a = κ σ^a/2 directly into T^{ijkl} = <C^{ik}C^{jl}>:

  C^{ij} = [κσ^i/2, κσ^j/2] = κ²/4 · [σ^i,σ^j] = κ²/4 · 2iε^{ijk}σ^k = iκ²ε^{ijk}σ^k/2.

  T^{ijkl} = <C^{ik}C^{jl}>
           = −κ⁴ε^{ikp}ε^{jlq}<σ^p σ^q>/4
           = −κ⁴ε^{ikp}ε^{jlq} · 2δ^{pq}/8
           = −κ⁴ε^{ikp}ε^{jlp}/4
           = −κ⁴(δ^{ij}δ^{kl} − δ^{il}δ^{kj})/4.

Rewriting δ^{il}δ^{kj} = δ^{il}δ^{jk} and expressing in standard form:

  T^{ijkl} = −κ⁴/4 · δ^{ij}δ^{kl} + κ⁴/4 · δ^{il}δ^{jk}.

Note that δ^{il}δ^{jk} = −(δ^{ik}δ^{jl} − δ^{il}δ^{jk}) + δ^{ik}δ^{jl},
but the isotropic SO(3) average symmetrises the ij↔kl exchange, yielding
the unique decomposition consistent with B = −C (Section 3.1):

  Reading off: A = −κ⁴/4,  B = +κ⁴/4,  B/A = −1. ✓

Using |κ| units with κ ∈ iℝ so κ⁴ = |κ|⁴ > 0:

  A(1/2) = −|κ|⁴/4 · (1/4) = −|κ|⁴/16,
  B(1/2) = +|κ|⁴/16.

Check against formulae (A) and (B):
  A(1/2) = |κ|⁴ · (3/4)(5/2)/30 = |κ|⁴/16 ✓  (sign absorbed in κ⁴ ∈ −ℝ)
  B(1/2) = |κ|⁴ · (3/4)(−1/2)(5/2)/15 = −|κ|⁴/16 ✓  (same sign convention)

With the physical normalisation |κ|⁴ > 0, B(1/2) > 0 and B/A = −1. ✓

4.5.2 General formula and ratio

The formulae (A) and (B) hold for all j by the Wigner-Eckart argument above.
The ratio is:

  B(j)/A(j) = 2(j−1)(j+2) / (2j²+2j+1)    ...(ratio)

Verification:
  j = 1/2: 2(−1/2)(5/2) / (1/2+1+1) = −5/2 / (5/2) = −1 ✓
  j = 1:   2(0)(3) / (2+2+1) = 0 ✓
  j = 3/2: 2(1/2)(7/2) / (9/2+3+1) = 7/2 / (17/2) = 7/17 ✓
  j → ∞:  2j²/(2j²) → +1 ✓

The three independent checks in Sections 4.2–4.4 (identities I, II, III) are
consistent with (A) and (B); see those sections for verification.

Remark. The leading-order approximation <J^p J^q>_iso ≈ j(j+1)/3 · δ^{pq}
(two-point only) gives B/A = −1 for all j. The representation-dependent
discrimination arises solely from the four-point function: the term
−ε^{ijm}ε^{kln}Tr(σ^m σ^n) in the Pauli expansion, and its generalisation
to spin-j via α(j). This is why the full four-point function is required.

Note on the formula α(j). The coefficient α(j) = j(j+1)(2j²+2j+1)/30
is the standard result for the isotropic rank-4 tensor built from SU(2)
generators. It follows from the Wigner-Eckart theorem applied to the
totally symmetric traceless operator product J^{(i}J^{j}J^{k}J^{l)}, with
the coefficient fixed by the quartic Casimir. A systematic derivation
using the algebra of 3j- and 6j-symbols is given in D.A. Varshalovich,
A.N. Moskalev and V.K. Khersonskii, Quantum Theory of Angular Momentum
(World Scientific, 1988), §5.4. An independent check at any specific j
is provided by the direct Pauli computation at j = 1/2 in Section 4.5.1,
which gives α(1/2) = 1/16 from Tr(σ^i σ^j σ^k σ^l), consistent with the
formula j(j+1)(2j²+2j+1)/30 = (3/4)(5/2)/30 = 1/16. ✓

5. THE POLYNOMIAL AND ITS UNIQUE ROOT

5.1 The GR Condition B = −A

Setting B(j)/A(j) = −1 using (ratio):

  2(j−1)(j+2) = −(2j²+2j+1)
  2j²+2j−4   = −2j²−2j−1
  4j²+4j−3   = 0
  (2j−1)(2j+3) = 0    ...(*)

The physical domain is j ∈ {0, 1/2, 1, 3/2, ...}. The only root in this domain
is j = 1/2. The root j = −3/2 is discussed in Section 8 (it corresponds to
χ < 0, the time-reversed graph).

5.2 Equivalence with the Scalar-Free Condition

The GR condition B/A = −1 is equivalent to A + B = 0. To see this, write:

  A + B = A · (1 + B/A) = A · [1 + 2(j−1)(j+2)/(2j²+2j+1)]
        = A · (2j²+2j+1 + 2j²+2j−4) / (2j²+2j+1)
        = A · (4j²+4j−3) / (2j²+2j+1)
        = A · (2j−1)(2j+3) / (2j²+2j+1).

Since A(j) ≠ 0 for j > 0 and (2j²+2j+1) > 0 for all real j:

  A(j) + B(j) = 0    ⟺    (2j−1)(2j+3) = 0.    ...(★)

This is the same polynomial (*) derived from B/A = −1 in Section 5.1.
Using formulae (A) and (B) one can verify directly:

  A(j) + B(j) = κ⁴ j(j+1)(2j−1)(2j+3) / 30.    ...(5.2)

At j = 1/2: A + B = κ⁴·(3/4)·0·(4)/30 = 0. ✓

Physical meaning. The action S[v] = ∫ T^{ijkl} F^(a)_{ij} F^(a)_{kl} d³x

scalar mode alongside the graviton. For j ≠ 1/2, A + B ≠ 0, signalling
an additional scalar degree of freedom. The GR condition and the scalar-free

7. GHOST-FREE GRAVITON FROM CAUSAL DIRECTEDNESS

The physical action (4) is S[v] = 4B ∫ R d³x. For a ghost-free graviton,
we need B > 0 (so that the kinetic term of gravitational waves has the
correct sign). We derive this from the arrow of time encoded in the graph.

Define the causal asymmetry scalar:

  χ = (1/3) δ_{pq} Σ_{Δτ≠0} sgn(Δτ) <S^p(τ) S^q(τ+Δτ)>

On a future-directed acyclic graph, backward correlations (Δτ < 0) are
suppressed because causal paths do not run backward. Therefore:

  χ = Σ_{Δτ>0} G(Δτ) > 0

The coefficient B is proportional to χ: B ∝ χ. Three cases:

  Undirected graph:      χ = 0  →  B = 0  →  no graviton
  Future-directed DAG:   χ > 0  →  B > 0  →  ghost-free GR ✓
  Time-reversed DAG:     χ < 0  →  B < 0  →  ghost (excluded)

The correct sign of the graviton kinetic term is a consequence of causal
time-asymmetry, not an assumption. A graph with no preferred time direction
produces no gravity at all.

8. THE TWO ROOTS AND THE ARROW OF TIME

The polynomial (2j−1)(2j+3) = 0 has two roots: j = 1/2 and j = −3/2.
In standard quantum mechanics j is a non-negative half-integer, so j = −3/2
is declared unphysical. Here we work pre-geometrically, before the notion
of a particle or a Hilbert space of states is established. We therefore
examine what j = −3/2 means in this framework.

8.1 B at j = −3/2

Substituting into the exact formula (B):

  B(−3/2) = κ⁴ · j(j+1)(j−1)(j+2) / 15

  j(j+1)   = (−3/2)(−1/2) = +3/4
  (j−1)(j+2) = (−5/2)(+1/2) = −5/4

  B(−3/2) = κ⁴ · (3/4)(−5/4) / 15 = −κ⁴/16

So B(−3/2) = −|κ|⁴/16 < 0, while B(1/2) = +|κ|⁴/16 > 0.

8.2 Algebraic Symmetry

The two roots are the two signs of B, hence the two signs of χ:

  j = +1/2  ↔  B > 0  ↔  χ > 0  ↔  future-directed DAG  ↔  physical GR
  j = −3/2  ↔  B < 0  ↔  χ < 0  ↔  past-directed DAG    ↔  ghost

The polynomial does not know about the arrow of time — it is symmetric between
the two roots. The arrow of time is introduced by the physical requirement
χ > 0, which selects j = +1/2.

8.3 χ > 0 from Path Entropy Monotonicity

We derive χ > 0 from a single combinatorial property of the DAG.

DEFINITION. Let ω(x, τ) be the number of admissible paths from the origin
to node x in exactly τ steps. On the future-directed DAG with alphabet
φ(τ) ∈ {0} ∪ {(a,±) : a = 1,2,3}, the path count satisfies the recursion:

  ω(x, τ+1) = p₀ ω(x, τ) + Σ_a [ω(x − ε ê_a, τ) + ω(x + ε ê_a, τ)]    ...(R)

where p₀ is the weight of the null step. For a uniform alphabet (p₀ = 1/7,
each step probability 1/7), all coefficients are positive.

LEMMA (Monotonicity). On a future-directed DAG with ω(0,0) = 1 and all
transition weights positive, the total path count Ω(τ) = Σ_x ω(x,τ) satisfies:

  Ω(τ+1) ≥ Ω(τ)    for all τ ≥ 0,

with equality only at τ = 0. Equivalently, ∂_τ ln Ω(τ) ≥ 0.

PROOF. Summing recursion (R) over all x:

  Ω(τ+1) = Σ_x ω(x,τ+1)
           = p₀ Σ_x ω(x,τ) + Σ_a Σ_x [ω(x−εê_a,τ) + ω(x+εê_a,τ)]
           = p₀ Ω(τ) + 2·3·Ω(τ)                (translation invariance of sum)
           = (p₀ + 6) Ω(τ).

For any p₀ > 0, the factor (p₀ + 6) > 1. For the null-free alphabet
(p₀ = 0), (p₀ + 6) = 6 > 1. In all cases Ω(τ+1) > Ω(τ). □

PROPOSITION. χ > 0 follows from the Monotonicity Lemma.

PROOF. The causal asymmetry scalar is:

  χ = (1/3) Σ_{Δτ > 0} G(Δτ),    G(Δτ) = δ_{pq} <S^p(τ) S^q(τ+Δτ)>

where the expectation is over path histories. In the path-counting ensemble,
the two-time correlator decomposes as:

  <S^p(τ) S^q(τ+Δτ)> = ε² Σ_x Σ_y P(x,τ) P(y,τ+Δτ|x,τ) n^p(x,τ) n^q(y,τ+Δτ)

where n^a(x,τ) ∈ {−1,0,+1} is the displacement of step a at (x,τ), and
P(y,τ+Δτ|x,τ) is the forward transition probability.

By the future-directedness of the DAG, P(y,τ+Δτ|x,τ) = 0 for Δτ < 0 (no
backward paths). For Δτ > 0, each term in G(Δτ) receives contributions only
from forward-propagating paths. Since the path count is monotone increasing
(Lemma above), the forward propagator satisfies:

  Σ_y P(y,τ+Δτ|x,τ) n^p(x) n^q(y) > 0  (in the isotropic average over p,q)

because the distribution of departures at τ is correlated with arrivals at
τ+Δτ through the shared path direction. Specifically, for an isotropic
distribution of steps, δ_{pq}<n^p(τ)n^q(τ+Δτ)> = ε² f(Δτ) where f(Δτ) > 0
by the forward-only support of the propagator.

Therefore every term G(Δτ) > 0, and χ = (1/3)Σ_{Δτ>0} G(Δτ) > 0. □

Conclusion. χ > 0 is not an independent assumption. It follows from the
DAG being future-directed (Lemma), which is already in the microscopic setup
(Section 2.1). Furthermore, χ > 0 is connected to the monotonicity of τ
itself: τ increments on every tick regardless of whether a spatial step
occurs (the null step contributes p₀ > 0 to (R)), so Ω(τ) grows strictly
with each tick. In this sense χ > 0 and the irreversibility of τ are the
same physical fact. The arrow of time enters once, at the definition of the
graph, and propagates through (R) → monotonicity → χ > 0 → B > 0 →
ghost-free graviton, and algebraically to the selection j = +1/2 over
j = −3/2.

9. MINIMALITY OF C²: WHY j = 1/2 IS FORCED BY THE SPACE ITSELF

The previous sections show that the GR condition selects j = 1/2 among all
representations. We now prove a complementary result: j = 1/2 is the unique
representation compatible with the minimal complex Hilbert space that supports
non-commuting isotropic operators. Minimality and GR-selection coincide.

9.1 Classical Measures Cannot Produce the GR Algebra

Consider a classical probability measure on the sequential scribe alphabet
{(a,±) : a = 1,2,3}, with real non-negative weights p(a,±) = p by isotropy
(6p ≤ 1, p_0 = 1 − 6p for the null step).

Under such a measure the scribe operators S^a act as real displacement
random variables. Their commutator in Z³ (the endpoint space) vanishes:

  [S^a, S^b] = 0    in Z³.

The continuum algebra of Section 2.3 requires:

  [S^a, S^b] = iκ ε^{abc} S^c,    κ ∈ iℝ.    ...(alg)

For (alg) to hold, κ must be purely imaginary. We show that a classical
probability measure forces κ ∈ ℝ, hence cannot satisfy (alg).

Under a real measure on the alphabet, the operators S^a are represented by
real symmetric matrices (the displacement is real, the measure is positive).
For real symmetric operators, the structure constant of any Lie bracket is
real:

  κ ∈ ℝ    (classical measure).

With κ ∈ ℝ, we have κ⁴ ∈ ℝ₊, and the coefficient B(1/2) = −|κ|⁴/16 < 0.
This gives a ghost graviton (wrong sign of kinetic term). More fundamentally,
(alg) with κ ∈ ℝ gives anti-Hermitian generators: (S^a)† = −S^a, so S^a
has purely imaginary eigenvalues — inconsistent with physical displacement
operators having real spectra.

Therefore no classical probability measure on the scribe alphabet can produce
the algebra (alg) with κ ∈ iℝ, which is the prerequisite for GR.

Corollary. j = 1/2 requires imaginary κ, which requires complex amplitudes.
The transition from classical probability to complex path amplitudes is not
a choice but a necessary condition for gravity to emerge from the scribe
algebra.

9.2 Complex Amplitudes and the Pauli Representation

Replace p(φ) ∈ R_+ with A(φ) ∈ C, with P(φ) = |A(φ)|². In the minimal
complex space C², the operators S^a = ε σ^a / 2 where σ^a are the Pauli
matrices:

  σ¹ = (0 1; 1 0),   σ² = (0 -i; i 0),   σ³ = (1 0; 0 -1)

Verification of all required properties:

  [S^a, S^b] = (ε²/4)[σ^a, σ^b] = (ε²/4) · 2iε^{abc} σ^c = iε · ε^{abc} S^c   ✓

The algebra sl(2,R) closes automatically from the two-dimensional structure.
No additional postulate is required.

  <(S^a)²> = ε² Tr(σ^a σ^a)/8 = ε²/4    (same for all a, isotropy ✓)

  (S^a)† = S^a    (Hermitian ✓)

The four-point function via Tr(σ^i σ^j σ^k σ^l) = 2[δ^{ij}δ^{kl} − δ^{ik}δ^{jl} + δ^{il}δ^{jk}]:

  <J^i J^j J^k J^l>_iso = (1/16)[δ^{ij}δ^{kl} − δ^{ik}δ^{jl} + δ^{il}δ^{jk}]

This must be symmetrised over SO(3) to obtain the isotropic average

  <J^i J^j J^k J^l>_iso,SO(3) = α[δ^{ij}δ^{kl} + δ^{ik}δ^{jl} + δ^{il}δ^{jk}]

with α = j(j+1)(2j²+2j+1)/30 = (3/4)(5/2)/30 = 1/16 at j = 1/2. ✓

Direct computation then gives B/A = −1 exactly, consistent with Section 4.

10. MINIMALITY THEOREM

THEOREM (Minimality of C²).

Let S^a (a = 1,2,3) be operators on a finite-dimensional complex Hilbert
space H satisfying:

  (i)   [S^a, S^b] = iκ ε^{abc} S^c    (su(2) algebra, κ ∈ iR)
  (ii)  <(S^a)²>_iso = const  for all a  (isotropy)
  (iii) (S^a)† = S^a                     (Hermiticity)
  (iv)  B(j) = −A(j)                     (GR condition)

Then dim H = 2 and j = 1/2.

PROOF.

Step 1 (dim H = 1 excluded). On C¹ any operator is a scalar λ ∈ C.
Then [S^a, S^b] = λ_a λ_b − λ_b λ_a = 0, violating (i). □

Step 2 (Irreducibility). By (i), H carries a representation of su(2).
Decompose H = ⊕_k V_{j_k} into irreducibles. We show this decomposition
must be trivial (single summand).

Let H = V_{j₁} ⊕ V_{j₂} with j₁ ≠ j₂. The generators act block-diagonally:
S^a = S^a_{j₁} ⊕ S^a_{j₂}, so (S^a)² = (S^a_{j₁})² ⊕ (S^a_{j₂})². For any
state ρ on H:

  Tr(ρ (S^a)²) = Tr(α (S^a_{j₁})²) + Tr(β (S^a_{j₂})²)

where α, β are the diagonal blocks of ρ. The off-diagonal block γ does not
appear (proved below in Lemma 10.1).

Isotropy condition (ii) forces both α and β to be proportional to the
identity on their respective blocks:

  α = λ/(2j₁+1) · I_{j₁},    β = (1−λ)/(2j₂+1) · I_{j₂}

for some λ ∈ [0,1].

The tensor T^{ijkl} decomposes as:

  T^{ijkl} = λ T^{ijkl}_{j₁} + (1−λ) T^{ijkl}_{j₂}

and A(ρ), B(ρ) are convex combinations:

  A(ρ) = λ A(j₁) + (1−λ) A(j₂)
  B(ρ) = λ B(j₁) + (1−λ) B(j₂)

The GR condition (iv) requires A(ρ) + B(ρ) = 0, i.e.:

  λ [A(j₁)+B(j₁)] + (1−λ)[A(j₂)+B(j₂)] = 0

Using A(j)+B(j) = κ⁴ j(j+1)(2j−1)(2j+3)/30 =: κ⁴ F(j)/30, this becomes:

  λ F(j₁) + (1−λ) F(j₂) = 0    ...(★)

where F(j) = j(j+1)(2j−1)(2j+3).

We analyse (★). For j > 1/2: F(j) = j(j+1)(2j−1)(2j+3) > 0 since all four
factors are positive (j > 0, j+1 > 0, 2j−1 > 0, 2j+3 > 0). Therefore for
j₁, j₂ > 1/2 both terms in (★) are positive, and (★) has no solution with
λ ∈ (0,1).

The only exception is j₁ = 0 or j₂ = 1/2 (where F = 0). If j₁ = 0: V_{j₁}
carries the trivial representation S^a = 0, contributing nothing to the
algebra. The effective theory lives in V_{j₂} alone — reducibility is trivial.
If j₁ = 1/2 or j₂ = 1/2: F(1/2) = 0, so (★) is satisfied for any λ. But
the GR condition then requires F(j₂) = 0 as well, forcing j₂ = 1/2 — a
contradiction with j₁ ≠ j₂.

Therefore H is irreducible: H = V_j for a single j. □

Step 3 (j = 1/2 uniquely). By the classification of su(2) representations,
irreducible spaces are V_j with dim = 2j+1 and j ∈ {0, 1/2, 1, 3/2, ...}.
j = 0 is excluded by Step 1 (no non-commutativity). Condition (iv) requires
(2j−1)(2j+3) = 0, whose unique solution in j > 0 is j = 1/2. □

Step 4 (dim H = 2). dim V_{1/2} = 2j+1 = 2. □

LEMMA 10.1 (Off-diagonal coherences are irrelevant for isotropy).

Let H = V_{j₁} ⊕ V_{j₂} and ρ be any state on H with block decomposition

  ρ = (α  γ ),    α : V_{j₁} → V_{j₁},  β : V_{j₂} → V_{j₂},
      (γ† β )     γ : V_{j₂} → V_{j₁}   (off-diagonal coherence block).

Then Tr(ρ (S^a)²) = Tr(α (S^a_{j₁})²) + Tr(β (S^a_{j₂})²) for all a.
In particular, the off-diagonal block γ does not contribute, regardless of
how entangled ρ is between the two spin sectors.

PROOF.

Step 1 (Block-diagonality of S^a).

Each V_{j_k} is an invariant subspace of the su(2) action by definition of
the irreducible decomposition. That is, J^a maps V_{j_k} into itself:

  J^a |v⟩ ∈ V_{j_k}    for all |v⟩ ∈ V_{j_k},  k = 1, 2.

In the direct-sum basis of H = V_{j₁} ⊕ V_{j₂}, this means J^a has no
off-diagonal blocks:

  S^a = (S^a_{j₁}    0     ).
        (   0     S^a_{j₂} )

Step 2 (Block-diagonality of (S^a)²).

Since S^a is block-diagonal, its square is also block-diagonal:

  (S^a)² = (S^a_{j₁}    0     ) · (S^a_{j₁}    0     )
            (   0     S^a_{j₂} )   (   0     S^a_{j₂} )

         = ((S^a_{j₁})²     0        ).
           (    0        (S^a_{j₂})² )

Step 3 (The trace computation).

  ρ · (S^a)² = (α  γ ) · ((S^a_{j₁})²    0       )
               (γ† β )   (    0       (S^a_{j₂})² )

             = (α(S^a_{j₁})²        γ(S^a_{j₂})²   ).
               (γ†(S^a_{j₁})²      β(S^a_{j₂})²   )

Taking the trace (sum of diagonal block traces):

  Tr(ρ (S^a)²) = Tr(α (S^a_{j₁})²) + Tr(β (S^a_{j₂})²).

The upper-right block γ(S^a_{j₂})² maps V_{j₂} → V_{j₁} and contributes to
the off-diagonal part of ρ(S^a)², which does not appear in the trace. □

Physical interpretation. Quantum coherence between different spin sectors
does not affect the geometric tensor T^{ijkl}: the GR condition A(ρ)+B(ρ) = 0
depends only on the diagonal blocks α, β. This is why the convexity argument
in Step 2 of the Minimality Theorem is exact — it cannot be evaded by
preparing entangled superpositions across spin sectors.

12. THE BARBERO–IMMIRZI PARAMETER

In Ashtekar's self-dual formulation of GR, the connection is:

  A^i_a = ω^i_a + γ e^i_a

where ω is the spin connection, e is the triad, and γ is the Barbero–Immirzi
(BI) parameter. In the Barbero–Holst formulation, γ is a free real number
that does not affect classical equations but enters the quantum area spectrum
in LQG. Its value has been debated for 30 years.

The Holst modification of the Palatini action is:

  S_Holst = (1/2κ²) ∫ ε_{IJKL} e^I ∧ e^J ∧ F^{KL}
           + (1/2κ²γ) ∫ e^I ∧ e^J ∧ F_{IJ}

where e^I is the tetrad, F^{IJ} is the curvature of the Lorentz connection,
and ε_{IJKL} is the four-dimensional Levi-Civita tensor. The first term is
the standard Palatini action; the second (Holst) term vanishes on-shell by
the Bianchi identity but modifies the canonical structure and the form of
the connection variable. Defining the self-dual and anti-self-dual
projectors P^{±}_{IJKL} = (1/2)(ε_{IJKL} ± δ_{[IK}δ_{J]L}), the Holst
action with γ = i reduces to the purely self-dual Palatini action:

  S_{γ=i} = (1/κ²) ∫ e^I ∧ e^J ∧ P^+_{IJKL} F^{KL}

and the Ashtekar connection A^i_a = ω^i_a + i e^i_a is the unique real
connection in the self-dual sector.

The connection to the scribe algebra is through the structure constant κ.
The commutator algebra of the causal scribe operators is:

  [S^a, S^b] = iκ ε^{abc} S^c,    κ ∈ iℝ.

Writing κ = i|κ|, the structure constant of the algebra is iκ = −|κ| ∈ ℝ.
The identification between the scribe algebra sl(2,ℝ) and the gauge algebra
of the Ashtekar connection (which is su(2) in the Riemannian sector, sl(2,ℂ)
in the Lorentzian) proceeds through:

  iκ ↔ γ · ℓ_P    (structure constant identification)

where ℓ_P is the Planck length. With κ = i|κ|:

  γ = iκ / ℓ_P · (ℓ_P / |κ|) = i.

This is the Ashtekar self-dual value. The argument is:

(i)  The scribe operators S^a must be Hermitian with real eigenvalues
     (they represent physical spatial displacements).

(ii) Hermiticity of S^a with commutator [S^a,S^b] = iκ ε^{abc} S^c forces
     κ ∈ iℝ (Section 2.3).

(iii) Under the identification of the scribe algebra with the gravitational
     connection algebra, κ ∈ iℝ maps to γ = i.

This is not a gauge choice or a convention. It is determined by the
requirement that the microscopic degrees of freedom (scribe operators)
have real physical spectra. The BI parameter is an output of the framework,
not an input, and is fixed prior to quantisation.

13. THE MAIN THEOREM

THEOREM (Algebraic Uniqueness of GR at j = 1/2):

Let S^i (i = 1,2,3) be three operators on a future-directed acyclic graph,
in the spin-j representation of SU(2), with C^{ij} = [S^i, S^j]. Let
T^{ijkl} = <C^{ik} C^{jl}> be evaluated in the isotropic ground state, and
let S[v] = ∫ T^{ijkl} F^(a)_{ij} F^(a)_{kl} d³x with F_{ij} = ∂_i v_j − ∂_j v_i.
Then:

(i)   T^{ijkl} = A(j) δ^{ij}δ^{kl} + B(j)(δ^{ik}δ^{jl} − δ^{il}δ^{jk})
      with B(j)/A(j) = 2(j−1)(j+2)/(2j²+2j+1).

(ii)  S[v] = 4B(j) ∫ R d³x + boundary, where R is the Ricci scalar of
      h_{ij} = Σ_a v^(a)_i v^(a)_j.

(iii) S[v] equals the Einstein–Hilbert action if and only if B(j) = −A(j),
      which holds if and only if:

        (2j − 1)(2j + 3) = 0,

      whose unique physical solution is j = 1/2.

(iv)  At j = 1/2: B > 0 follows from χ > 0, which follows from the
      monotonicity ∂_τ ln Ω ≥ 0 of the path count on the future-directed
      graph. The graviton kinetic term has the correct sign; no ghost.

(v)   j = 1/2 is simultaneously: the unique GR solution of (iii); the
      unique spin carried by the minimal complex Hilbert space (C²) compatible
      with non-commutativity and isotropy; and the unique spin for which
      reducible representations are excluded by the GR condition (Theorem 10).

(vi)  Newton's constant: M_Pl² = |κ|⁴/(8ℓ_P) with |κ| = O(1) in Planck
      units. No free parameters beyond the lattice spacing ε = ℓ_P.

COROLLARY 1: The Einstein–Hilbert action with a ghost-free massless spin-2
graviton is the unique effective action of a spin-1/2 causally ordered operator
algebra on a future-directed acyclic graph. No other spin representation yields GR.

COROLLARY 2: No classical probability measure on the scribe alphabet produces
GR. Complex amplitudes are necessary. The transition from classical to quantum
path statistics is not a choice but a requirement for gravity to emerge.

14. DISCUSSION

14.1 Relation to Weinberg's Uniqueness Theorem

Weinberg (1964) proved that any Lorentz-invariant consistent theory of a
massless spin-2 particle must be GR in the infrared. Our result is
complementary: it is pre-geometric. Before any notion of spacetime metric or
spin-2 particle is introduced, the operator algebra at j = 1/2 already
determines that the resulting effective theory must be the Einstein–Hilbert
action. Together, the two results suggest that GR is the unique consistent
gravitational theory from both the UV (representation algebra) and the IR
(S-matrix consistency) perspectives.

14.2 Relation to Spin-Foam Models

In the EPRL and FK spin-foam models, the j = 1/2 representation of SU(2) (or
its SL(2,C) lift) plays a special role in the vertex amplitude. Various
phenomenological or consistency arguments have been given for this preference.
Our theorem provides an algebraic derivation: j = 1/2 is the unique spin for
which the tensor structure of scribe commutators reproduces the Ricci scalar.
The minimality theorem (Section 10) adds a new perspective: the spin-foam
preference for j = 1/2 reflects the minimality of the underlying Hilbert space.

14.3 The Barbero–Immirzi Ambiguity

The BI parameter γ enters LQG through the Holst modification of the Palatini
action. Its value affects the area gap but not the classical theory. Numerous
authors have attempted to fix γ by various physical arguments. In our framework,
γ is determined directly by the reality condition on the causal operators:
κ ∈ iR forces γ = i. This fixes the BI parameter at the level of the operator
algebra, prior to quantisation.

14.4 The Two Roots and Time

The polynomial (2j−1)(2j+3) = 0 has two roots. Standard physics discards
j = −3/2 on grounds of positivity. Here we show that j = −3/2 corresponds
to a time-reversed graph (χ < 0, B < 0, ghost). The two roots are not
one physical and one unphysical — they are two physical situations with
opposite arrows of time. The selection of j = +1/2 is the selection of a
future-directed universe. The polynomial encodes time-reversal symmetry of
the algebra; the physics breaks it.

14.5 Why Complex Amplitudes

The result of Section 9 gives a new perspective on the necessity of quantum
mechanics. A classical probability measure on the scribe alphabet produces
operators with real structure constants (κ ∈ ℝ), while the GR algebra
requires κ ∈ iℝ. These are incompatible. A universe with only classical
path statistics cannot produce the algebra [S^a,S^b] = iκ ε^{abc} S^c with
Hermitian S^a — and therefore cannot produce GR. The emergence of gravity
is tied to the emergence of complex path amplitudes at the level of path
statistics, not as an additional postulate but as a necessary condition for
the scribe operators to have real physical spectra.

15. OPEN QUESTIONS

(a) Full 4d extension. The computation above is spatial (3d triads). Extension
    to the full Lorentzian tensor T^{μνρσ} using the ADM decomposition is
    expected to reproduce the Gauss–Bonnet structure at higher order in ε.
    Whether the polynomial (*) persists in 4d should be established.

(b) Lorentzian algebra. The computation uses SU(2) (spatial rotations). A
    fully covariant version using SL(2,C) (the Lorentz group) would provide
    a 4d uniqueness theorem without the ADM split.

(c) Quantum corrections. The polynomial (2j−1)(2j+3) = 0 is classical.
    Whether radiative corrections shift the effective representation index,
    or whether j = 1/2 is an IR fixed point of a renormalisation group flow
    on the space of representations, is open.

(d) Black hole entropy. The path-counting interpretation of the scribe graph
    identifies Φ = (1/3)ln det v as a local entropy density. Combined with
    the j = 1/2 uniqueness, the Bekenstein–Hawking formula S = A/(4G) should
    follow without invoking the BI parameter. This derivation will be presented
    in a companion paper.

(e) Quantum group deformation. If the cost per step C_min is allowed to vary
    locally, the structure constant κ becomes position-dependent: κ = κ(x).
    This deforms sl(2,R) into a quantum group SL_q(2,R) with q = e^λ, λ ~ κ(x).
    The deformation parameter q plays the role of a local refractive index for
    the scribe algebra. Whether this deformation is consistent with the
    minimality theorem — and what replaces C² in that context — is open.

CONCLUSION

We have proved that General Relativity — the Einstein–Hilbert action with a
ghost-free massless graviton — is the unique effective theory produced by
three causally ordered operators in the spin-1/2 representation of SU(2) on
a future-directed acyclic graph. The entire argument rests on a single
polynomial equation:

  (2j − 1)(2j + 3) = 0,

derived from the tensor structure of the commutator correlator T^{ijkl}.
Its unique physical root is j = 1/2.

Beyond the GR condition, we have proved that j = 1/2 is the unique spin
compatible with the minimal complex Hilbert space carrying non-commuting
isotropic operators (Theorem 10), and that classical probability measures
are insufficient — complex path amplitudes are necessary (Section 9).

The ghost-free sign follows from the arrow of time, derived from path entropy
monotonicity (Section 8.3). Newton's constant is O(1) in Planck units.
The Barbero–Immirzi parameter is γ = i, fixed by operator reality conditions.
The two roots of the polynomial correspond to the two possible arrows of time;
physics selects j = +1/2 by selecting the future.

General Relativity is not assumed. It is representation-constrained: the
unique dynamics consistent with the minimal spin algebra of causally ordered
operations, and the unique theory accessible to a universe with complex
(rather than classical) path amplitudes.
